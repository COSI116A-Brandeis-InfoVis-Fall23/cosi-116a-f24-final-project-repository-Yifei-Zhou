Project-long Course Project as part of COSI 116A: Information Visualization, taught by Prof. Dylan Cashman, Brandeis University.

Motivation

Our project focuses on analyzing the Boston Public Transportation system through data visualization, with the goal of uncovering insights into traffic congestion, subway ridership patterns, and efficiency comparisons with other cities. We aim to understand how well Boston public transport serves its population, especially in comparison to other cities like New York and Chicago. By using this data, we hope to provide actionable insights for improving public transportation and reducing traffic in Boston. The project will use various datasets related to subway lines, ridership, delays, and traffic congestion.

Our team is dedicated to exploring the role of public transportation in reducing traffic congestion in major cities, with a specific focus on Boston's subway lines and a comparative analysis of public transportation efficiency across different cities.

Project Goals:

  1. Analyze How Public Transportation Reduces Traffic Congestion:
    • Investigate strategies employed by major cities to leverage public transport in easing traffic.
    • Assess the effectiveness of these strategies in real-world scenarios.
  2. Examine the Relationship Between Boston Subway Lines and Ridership:
    • Study ridership data across different lines.
    • Identify factors influencing ridership trends.
  3. Compare Public Transportation Efficiency Between Cities:
    • Select benchmark cities for comparison (e.g., New York, Chicago, San Francisco).
    • Evaluate key performance indicators like frequency, coverage, and user satisfaction.

Fall 2023 MBTA Rail Ridership Data by SDP Time Period, Route/Line, and Stop

Source: Massachusetts geoDOT

The dataset used for this project contains MBTA rail ridership data by route, time period, and stop. The key columns include route_name, time_period_name, average_flow, and other attributes detailing public transit information. The dataset captures ridership statistics across various subway lines, including metrics for different time periods throughout the day.

The data types primarily consist of categorical variables (route_name, time_period_name) and numerical variables (average_flow). The categorical data represents the subway lines and time intervals, while the numerical data represents ridership counts (average flow).

During data preprocessing, we grouped the data by route_name and time_period_name to analyze ridership trends across different subway lines and time periods. We calculated the mean average_flow for each combination of route and time period, while also ensuring logical sorting of the time periods (e.g., VERY_EARLY_MORNING, AM_PEAK, MIDDAY_SCHOOL) to accurately reflect the order of events throughout a day. Additionally, the OFF_PEAK category was excluded to focus on the more significant ridership periods. The preprocessing steps aimed to clean, structure, and format the data for effective visualization and analysis.

Fall 2023 MBTA Monthly Ridership Data by Mode

Source: Massachusetts geoDOT

The dataset used in this project consists of monthly MBTA ridership data broken down by transit mode, sourced from the file MBTA_Monthly_Ridership_By_Mode.csv. Each record provides a snapshot of monthly weekday ridership, identified by a service_date field formatted as “YYYY/MM,” a mode field indicating the type of transit service (e.g., bus, rapid transit, commuter rail, ferry), and a total_monthly_weekday_ridership field containing numeric ridership counts. This structure allows for detailed time-series analysis of how different transit modes perform over various months.

Before visualization and analysis, the data undergoes several preprocessing steps to ensure it is appropriately structured and typed. The service_date strings are parsed into JavaScript Date objects using d3.timeParse("%Y/%m"), facilitating accurate time-based plotting. Additionally, the total_monthly_weekday_ridership values, initially loaded as strings, are converted into numerical types, enabling proper scaling, axis generation, and arithmetic operations in the subsequent visualization.

Following these conversions, the dataset is grouped by mode and sorted chronologically by service_date. This grouping and sorting ensure that each mode’s data forms a continuous time series, ready for line chart plotting. As a result, the final processed dataset is clean, consistently typed, and logically organized, allowing researchers to straightforwardly compare monthly ridership trends across various MBTA transit modes.

Task Analysis

These visualizations are designed to help stakeholders—such as transportation planners, transit agencies, and policy analysts—understand patterns in MBTA ridership data, enabling them to make informed decisions about resource allocation, scheduling, and improvements to the transit system.

The tasks supported by these visualizations include identifying long-term trends in ridership across different transportation modes, pinpointing peak usage times and routes for rail services, and examining spatial-temporal patterns in ridership volume at individual stops. Goals at the domain level involve informing decision-making about service adjustments, enhancing reliability, and improving the rider experience. The “monthlyridershipbymode” charts allow users to compare how different modes (e.g., buses, commuter rail, subways) fluctuate over months or years, revealing seasonal factors or policy impacts. The “railridershipbytime” visualization focuses on the temporal dimension, showing how average ridership changes during various time periods of the day or week, enabling planners to refine scheduling and predict demand. Finally, the “ridershipheatmap” emphasizes correlation between stops and specific time frames, visually highlighting where and when ridership is most intense, thus guiding improvements in station facilities or targeted marketing efforts.

From these capabilities, users can abstract tasks such as filtering data to isolate particular modes or time periods, brushing and zooming to focus on interesting data segments, and highlighting to compare specific routes or stops. On a higher level, the processes supported by these tools lead to more effective strategic planning and operational adjustments. Ultimately, by making it easier to analyze complex ridership information, these visualizations help transportation authorities achieve the larger goal of creating a more efficient, rider-focused transit network.

Conclusion

Throughout this project, we explored MBTA ridership patterns using various visualization techniques to uncover meaningful insights. We examined long-term trends across multiple transportation modes, identified temporal usage patterns within the rail network, and highlighted where and when ridership is most concentrated through heatmaps. These visualizations supported interactive tasks like zooming, brushing, highlighting, and filtering—enabling transportation planners, policy analysts, and other stakeholders to navigate complex datasets more efficiently and derive actionable recommendations for system improvements.

Looking forward, there are several avenues to enhance this work. Incorporating more granular, real-time data would allow for analyzing dynamic demand fluctuations and adjusting strategies on the fly. Adding geo-spatial mapping layers with interactive route-planning features could provide further context for congestion hot-spots, while integrating rider feedback surveys and operational costs would enable a richer understanding of value and satisfaction. Finally, applying machine learning or predictive modeling techniques to the underlying data could help anticipate future demand patterns, guiding long-term strategic decisions and infrastructure investments.
